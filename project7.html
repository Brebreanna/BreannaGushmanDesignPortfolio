SnoozeControl:
Drowsy Driver Detection

SnoozeControl is my Senior Design Project — I joined as an engineer and took on the UX/UI work independently, then built and shipped the full-stack implementation of my own designs. Teammates handled ML model development and hardware integration.

01

The Problem

Drowsy driving contributes to an estimated 91,000 crashes annually in the United States. Yet consumer-facing solutions are limited to expensive aftermarket hardware or generic wellness apps that rely on self-reporting rather than objective detection.

Core challenge

Drivers need real-time intervention during drowsiness episodes, but existing solutions either don't detect drowsiness accurately, require too much user interaction, or fail to deliver alerts that actually wake severely tired drivers.

02

User Research

I conducted a mixed-methods pilot study with 12 licensed drivers, supplemented by academic literature review and an expert consultation with Dr. Shannon Roberts, a transportation safety researcher at UMass Amherst.

Key Findings

Finding 01 83.3%

of participants have driven while drowsy, yet only 8.3% regularly pull over to rest.

Finding 02 75%

prioritized loud audible alerts, but wanted them sustained — not just brief beeps.

Finding 03 83.3%

rated common coping strategies (caffeine, windows, music) as only "somewhat effective" or worse.

Finding 04 33.3%

expressed skepticism about camera monitoring due to privacy and liability concerns.

How Research Shaped Design

Research Finding Design Decision
Audio should "play for extended period vs. just beeps" Sustained 10–15 second alarm loops with escalating volume, requiring deliberate dismissal
Multi-modal alerts proven most effective (Dr. Roberts + academic research) Triple-layer: 110dB audio + rhythmic haptics + full-screen visual takeover
83.3% feel drowsy late at night; 58.3% on long highway drives Contextual detection modes with heightened sensitivity during high-risk windows
Only 8.3% pull over; coping strategies fail Alert triggers rest stop locator showing nearest safe pull-off with ETA
Privacy concern for 33%; fear of unreliability Zero data retention, all on-device processing, user calibration before driving

05

Usability Testing

I tested three core flows with senior engineering faculty — all licensed drivers — using low-fidelity prototypes with zero instruction or guidance.

✓
Core flows: fast and error-free

All three participants completed each task in 3–5 seconds with zero mistakes. One evaluator noted: "The UI is very simple and feels feasible for a real application."

→
History screen lacked depth

All evaluators expected trend data, not just a session log. Response: added interactive line graphs, trend indicators, and tap-to-expand session breakdowns.

→
EAR/MAR terminology was confusing

Technical metric labels (EAR: 0.23) were meaningless to non-engineers. Response: designed contextual tooltips explaining metrics in plain language.

06

Design & Development

After validating flows, I designed the high-fidelity interface in Figma and implemented the complete frontend and backend in React Native.

  • Minimal interaction model One tap to start monitoring. No interaction needed during active sessions unless an alert fires.
  • Multi-layered alert system Sustained 110dB audio (10–15 sec) + rhythmic haptics + full-screen visual warning that requires deliberate dismissal.
  • Progressive disclosure EAR/MAR metrics hidden by default with educational tooltips available for users who want to understand detection mechanics.
  • Privacy-transparent onboarding Screens explicitly communicate local-only processing — zero cloud uploads, zero data storage.
  • Behavioral insights dashboard Interactive trend graphs replace static logs, surfacing drowsiness patterns across time and driving conditions.
07

What I Learned

01 Test early with lo-fi

Faculty testing caught structural issues before I invested in visual polish, saving significant time.

02 Context shapes every decision

Designing for drivers means every extra tap or confusing label carries real safety consequences.

03 Research closes the perception gap

Users thought brief beeps would wake them. Research proved sustained multi-modal alerts are necessary.

04 Privacy requires proactive design

Building a secure system isn't enough — users need transparency built into the interface itself.

SnoozeControl shows that effective UX isn't just about what users say they want — it's about understanding what research shows actually works, then making it simple enough to use at 70 mph.
