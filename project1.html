The Global Conservatory:
Website Redesign

Redesigning the digital presence of an international music education organization — from outdated and hard to navigate, to modern, unified, and audience-first.

Role UX Designer
Scope Web + Social
Deliverables 3 page redesigns
Tools Figma, Canva

01

Context

When I joined the team, the organization was in the middle of a major digital transformation — redesigning key public-facing pages and building new ones from scratch. The existing Global Conservatory materials were outdated, hard to navigate, and underperforming in engagement. The Iceland program had no digital presence at all.

My focus

Create a unified online presence that reflected the organization's mission, improved navigation for a diverse international audience, and clearly communicated each program's unique offerings to students, collaborators, and donors.

02

Scope of Work

I fully owned the creative direction, design, and execution across three distinct projects simultaneously.

🌍 Global Conservatory — Redesign

Audited existing pages for navigation issues, visual inconsistencies, and content gaps. Rebuilt layouts with updated typography, colors, and imagery.

🏔️ Iceland Program — New Build

Created the full design from scratch with no existing materials — only project goals and provided information to work from.

🎵 Music Club — Visual Refresh

Contributed to improving the main site's visual presentation and strengthening the overall brand consistency.

📱 Social Media Content

Designed promotional content to increase reach and engagement, aligned with the refreshed brand style across platforms.

03

Challenges

Each project had its own personality — academic, travel-focused, and community-based — and I needed to unify the branding without flattening what made each one distinct.

Complexity
  • Three different audiences: faculty, students, international visitors
  • Design, content, and development phases overlapping
  • Building one project from zero while redesigning others
How I handled it
  • Shared wireframes early to catch misalignment before full execution
  • Kept communication open across teams as timelines shifted
  • Worked iteratively to keep visual and functional goals aligned
04

Process

  • Discovery and audit Reviewed existing pages to identify navigation issues, visual inconsistencies, and content gaps. Met with team members to understand priorities and how each project needed to differ in tone.
  • Early wireframes shared for feedback Built initial directions in Figma and shared them before moving into full execution — making adjustments early rather than late.
  • Full design execution Created complete layouts with updated typography, colors, and imagery tailored to each project's personality. Designed social content in parallel.
  • Handoff and live review Organized files for the technical team and reviewed live builds against the design vision before launch.
05

Outcomes

✓
Navigation improvements

Users could reach key details faster across all redesigned pages.

✓
Unified brand across three distinct projects

Academic, travel-focused, and community-based pages felt cohesive without losing their individual character.

✓
Leadership praised the clarity and modern look

Feedback highlighted the visual upgrade and improved content organization as clear wins.

✓
Social media reach increased

Content designed for the campaign reached a broader audience and improved engagement metrics.

→
Architecture adopted, visual direction adapted

The live site follows the page structure and UX system I designed. The team later switched from my original dark theme to a white background — reinforcing that strong UX foundations outlast any single visual direction.

06

What I Learned

01 One brand, multiple personalities

Designing across academic, travel, and community contexts in parallel sharpened my ability to adjust tone and visual language without losing coherence.

02 UX foundations outlast visual decisions

The team changed the color scheme after launch — but the page architecture held. Good structure survives style changes.

03 Early sharing prevents late rework

Sharing wireframes before full execution meant feedback arrived when changes were cheap, not after hours of polished work.

04 Web and social are one system

Designing both in parallel forced a level of visual consistency I wouldn't have achieved treating them as separate deliverables.

This internship taught me that design at an organization isn't just about screens — it's about building a system that holds up long after you've moved on.
