Cookr:
Meal Planning for Students

A swipe-based recipe app designed to turn "what's in my fridge?" into a meal — fast, fun, and built for tight budgets.

Role Solo UX Designer
Timeline 24 hours
Team 4 members
Tools Figma

01

The Problem

⏱ 24-hour hackathon constraint

College students stare at a half-stocked fridge with no idea what to make. Between classes, work, and tight budgets, meal planning becomes one more stressor rather than something enjoyable.

Design goal

Make deciding what to cook fast, fun, and affordable — with a swipe-based interaction borrowed from dating apps to reduce decision fatigue and make browsing feel effortless.

Primary Persona

Dove Busy student · Works part-time
  • Needs a low-friction path from "what's in my fridge?" to "I'm cooking this"
  • Limited budget — wants affordable, realistic recipes
  • Gets discouraged by apps that assume cooking expertise
  • Wants something encouraging, not overwhelming
02

Approach

I led design from ideation to high-fidelity mockups, collaborating with engineers in Figma and tracking tasks in Notion. With only 24 hours, I skipped wireframes entirely and moved straight to hi-fi — prioritizing fast, high-confidence decisions over process.

I evaluated Tasty, Mealime, and HelloFresh to identify what worked and where the gaps were.

What worked in competitors
  • Strong visual recipe cards
  • Inclusive dietary filtering
  • Clear step-by-step instructions
Where the gaps were
  • Assume prior cooking knowledge
  • No swipe-based discovery
  • Decision fatigue from large catalogs
  • Not designed for budget constraints
03

Design Decisions

Six screens, each solving a specific friction point in the student cooking journey.

👋 Onboarding

Lightweight flow with large rounded controls to collect dietary goals without friction.

🃏 Recipe Cards

Cook time, calories, and dietary icons visible at a glance for fast scanning.

👆 Swipe Feed

Vertical, minimalist browsing modeled after dating apps to minimize choice overload.

📋 Recipe Detail

Step-by-step instructions with clear hierarchy designed for in-kitchen use.

✅ Cooking Mode

Action-based checklist with high contrast and generous spacing for hands-free use.

🛒 Grocery List

Saved recipes automatically generate a shopping list to close the planning loop.

Core Design Principles

  • Personalization from the start Onboarding collects dietary goals and preferences so the feed feels relevant immediately.
  • Cultural inclusivity Diverse cuisines and dietary needs represented throughout to avoid the "default American" recipe trap.
  • Clarity and warmth Soft palette, playful icons, and large tap targets make the app feel inviting rather than clinical.
  • Accessibility baked in High contrast throughout, generous spacing, and large touch targets for one-handed use on smaller phones.
04

Outcome

In 24 hours I delivered a complete high-fidelity mobile prototype covering six polished screens. Cookr was praised for its clean UI, playful visuals, and focused scope. I owned all UX and UI decisions and delivered prototypes early to keep the engineering team unblocked throughout the hackathon.

What landed well

The swipe interaction felt intuitive and supported both casual browsing and focused in-kitchen cooking flows without switching modes.

05

What I Learned

01 Clarity over complexity

Under time pressure, cutting scope early freed me to execute the essentials really well instead of doing everything poorly.

02 Touch targets matter

Even small icon sizing decisions have real accessibility implications — larger buttons would improve one-handed use on smaller phones.

03 Design for the engineers

Delivering prototypes early kept development moving. Design decisions that ignore feasibility slow the whole team down.

04 What comes next

An image-recognition feature — snap your ingredients, get recipe suggestions — would expand utility while keeping the playful tone intact.

Cookr proved that strong UX decisions made fast still hold up — clarity of focus beats breadth of features, especially when the clock is running.
